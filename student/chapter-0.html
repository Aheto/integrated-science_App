üìò Understanding Solids, Liquids, and Gases

Everything around you‚Äîyour desk, water, the air you breathe‚Äîis made of matter. Matter exists in three main forms, called states of matter: solids, liquids, and gases. We can tell them apart by how they look, feel, and behave.

üîç Observing Physical Properties

Before classifying, scientists observe key properties:

  • Shape: Does it keep its own shape?
  • Volume: Does it take up a fixed amount of space?
  • Flow: Can it pour or spread out?
  • Texture: Is it rough, smooth, hard, soft?

üß± Solids

Solids have a fixed shape and fixed volume. Their particles are packed tightly together and vibrate in place.

Examples from your environment: wood, stone, metal spoon, book, plastic bottle (empty), sand.

‚úÖ Try this: Press a rock. Does it change shape? No! That‚Äôs a solid.

üíß Liquids

Liquids have a fixed volume but no fixed shape‚Äîthey take the shape of their container. Their particles are close but can slide past one another.

Examples: water, milk, oil, juice, blood, rain.

‚úÖ Try this: Pour water from a cup into a bowl. What changes? The shape‚Äîbut not the amount!

üí® Gases

Gases have no fixed shape and no fixed volume. They spread out to fill any container. Their particles move freely and quickly.

Examples: air, oxygen, carbon dioxide, steam, cooking gas, helium in balloons.
